## 1. Website Architecture

### 1.1 Tech Stack
- **Framework:** Single-page React application (JSX artifact)
- **Styling:** Tailwind CSS utility classes
- **State Management:** React useState and useReducer hooks
- **Deployment:** Self-contained single-file artifact, no external dependencies beyond Tailwind and React

### 1.2 Page Structure
The website is a single-page app with two main views, toggled via a top navigation bar:

**View 1 — Term Definitions:** All 35 terms organized into four collapsible sections (Road to War, Asian Theater, European Theater, Post-WWII Order). Each term expands to show its full definition. A search/filter bar at the top allows instant filtering across all terms.

**View 2 — Practice Quiz:** A 21-question multiple-choice quiz. Questions are presented one at a time with A/B/C/D options. After answering, the correct answer and a detailed explanation are revealed. A progress bar tracks completion. At the end, a score summary shows results with the option to review missed questions or retake the quiz.

### 1.3 UI Design Principles
- Clean, readable layout with generous whitespace
- Dark navy (#1F3864) as the primary accent color
- Light gray background with white content cards
- Sans-serif typography (system font stack)
- Responsive design that works on both desktop and mobile
- Subtle hover effects and smooth transitions for interactive elements
- No excessive animations or visual noise

## 2. Complete Term Definitions
Below is the full text content for every term, organized by section. Each definition is written at an AP-level depth, emphasizing historical significance, causation, and connections to broader themes.

### Road to War

#### Mukden Incident (1931)
A staged explosion along the Japanese-controlled South Manchuria Railway near Mukden (modern Shenyang), carried out by Japanese military officers as a false-flag pretext to invade and occupy Manchuria. The League of Nations condemned Japan's aggression, but its inability to enforce consequences exposed the League's fundamental weakness and emboldened future aggressors.

#### Manchukuo (1932–1945)
The Japanese puppet state established in Manchuria after the Mukden Incident, nominally headed by Puyi, the last Qing emperor. In reality, Japan controlled all political, economic, and military decisions. Manchukuo served as a resource base for Japanese industrialization and a model for Japan's broader imperial ambitions in East Asia under the "Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere."

#### Lebensraum
German for "living space," this concept was central to Nazi ideology and Hitler's foreign policy. Rooted in social Darwinism and racial theory, it held that the German "Aryan" race needed to expand eastward—particularly into Poland and the Soviet Union—to secure agricultural land and resources. Lebensraum provided the ideological justification for German territorial aggression and the eventual invasion of the USSR.

#### Remilitarization of the Rhineland (1936)
Hitler's decision to send German troops into the Rhineland, a demilitarized buffer zone between Germany and France established by the Treaty of Versailles. France and Britain protested but took no military action, calculating that the political cost of confrontation was too high. This non-response was a critical turning point: it validated Hitler's strategy of calculated risk-taking and demonstrated that the Western democracies would not enforce Versailles by force.

#### Nuremberg Laws (1935)
A pair of antisemitic laws passed at the annual Nazi Party rally in Nuremberg. The Reich Citizenship Law stripped Jews of German citizenship, and the Law for the Protection of German Blood and Honor prohibited marriages and sexual relations between Jews and non-Jewish Germans. These laws codified racial discrimination into law and marked a major escalation from social persecution to state-sponsored, legalized oppression of Jews.

#### Anschluss (March 1938)
The annexation of Austria into Nazi Germany, achieved through a combination of political pressure, Nazi agitation within Austria, and the threat of military invasion. Austrian chancellor Schuschnigg was forced to resign, and German troops marched in unopposed. A subsequent plebiscite (held under Nazi supervision) showed overwhelming support. The Anschluss violated the Treaty of Versailles but again met no military response from the Western powers.

#### Munich Conference (September 1938)
A meeting among Hitler, Mussolini, British PM Neville Chamberlain, and French PM Édouard Daladier to resolve the Sudeten Crisis. The agreement permitted Germany to annex the Sudetenland (the German-speaking border region of Czechoslovakia) in exchange for Hitler's promise of no further territorial demands. Czechoslovakia was not invited to the negotiations. Chamberlain declared it secured "peace for our time," but Hitler violated the agreement within six months by occupying the rest of Czechoslovakia.

#### Appeasement
The diplomatic strategy pursued primarily by Britain and France in the 1930s of making concessions to aggressive dictators (especially Hitler) in order to avoid war. Proponents argued it bought time for rearmament and reflected public war-weariness after WWI. Critics argue it emboldened Hitler by signaling that aggression would go unpunished. After the failure of the Munich Agreement, appeasement became widely discredited as a foreign policy approach.

#### Nazi-Soviet Non-Aggression Pact (August 1939)
A stunning agreement between Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union, signed by foreign ministers Ribbentrop and Molotov. Publicly, both nations pledged not to attack each other. A secret protocol divided Eastern Europe into spheres of influence: Germany would take western Poland and Lithuania, while the USSR would take eastern Poland, Finland, Estonia, Latvia, and Bessarabia. The pact freed Hitler to invade Poland without fear of a two-front war and shocked the Western democracies.

#### Axis Powers
The military alliance of Nazi Germany, Fascist Italy, and Imperial Japan, formalized through the Tripartite Pact of September 1940. The alliance was built on shared opposition to the existing international order, expansionist ambitions, and anti-communist ideology (though the Nazi-Soviet Pact temporarily complicated this). Hungary, Romania, and Bulgaria later joined as junior partners.

#### Allied Powers
The coalition opposing the Axis, anchored by Great Britain, the Soviet Union, the United States, and Free France. The "Grand Alliance" was forged not by shared ideology but by the common threat of Axis aggression. Internal tensions (especially between the Western democracies and Stalin's USSR) shaped wartime strategy and would ultimately fracture into Cold War rivalry after 1945.

### Asian Theater

#### Rape of Nanjing (December 1937 – January 1938)
The mass atrocity committed by Japanese Imperial Army troops after capturing the Chinese Nationalist capital of Nanjing. Over approximately six weeks, Japanese soldiers murdered an estimated 200,000–300,000 Chinese civilians and prisoners of war and committed widespread sexual violence against tens of thousands of women. The massacre demonstrated the extreme brutality of Japan's war in China and remains a deeply contested issue in East Asian historical memory and Sino-Japanese relations.

#### Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941)
A surprise Japanese military attack on the U.S. naval base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii, intended to cripple the American Pacific Fleet and buy time for Japanese expansion across Southeast Asia. The attack sank or damaged 8 battleships and killed over 2,400 Americans. Rather than demoralizing the U.S., Pearl Harbor unified American public opinion and ended isolationist resistance. The next day, Congress declared war on Japan, bringing the U.S. fully into WWII.

#### Island Hopping
The American military strategy in the Pacific Theater of selectively attacking certain Japanese-held islands while bypassing others that were heavily fortified. The goal was to capture islands that could serve as air and naval bases, allowing forces to advance steadily toward the Japanese home islands. Key battles in this campaign included Guadalcanal, Tarawa, Saipan, Iwo Jima, and Okinawa. Each battle demonstrated the ferocity of Japanese resistance and the high human cost of the Pacific war.

#### Firebombing of Tokyo (March 1945)
Operation Meetinghouse, a massive incendiary bombing raid on Tokyo carried out by the U.S. Army Air Forces on the night of March 9–10, 1945. Approximately 100,000 civilians were killed and 16 square miles of the city were destroyed, making it the single deadliest air raid in history. The campaign targeted Japan's largely wooden urban areas and reflected a shift toward strategic bombing aimed at destroying civilian morale and industrial capacity.

#### Hiroshima and Nagasaki (August 6 and 9, 1945)
The U.S. atomic bombings of two Japanese cities using nuclear weapons. "Little Boy" (uranium bomb) was dropped on Hiroshima on August 6, killing an estimated 80,000 instantly and up to 140,000 by year's end. "Fat Man" (plutonium bomb) hit Nagasaki on August 9, killing approximately 40,000 instantly. The bombings remain deeply controversial: supporters argue they shortened the war and avoided a costly invasion; critics contend they constituted an unnecessary use of force against civilian populations, especially given Japan's deteriorating military position and the Soviet declaration of war.

#### V-J Day (August 15 / September 2, 1945)
Victory over Japan Day, marking the end of World War II. August 15, 1945 is when Emperor Hirohito announced Japan's surrender via radio broadcast (the first time most Japanese citizens heard the emperor's voice). September 2, 1945 is the date of the formal surrender ceremony aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay. The two dates reflect the distinction between the announcement and the legal conclusion of the war.

### European Theater

#### Blitzkrieg
German for "lightning war," a military strategy combining fast-moving armor (Panzer divisions), motorized infantry, close air support (especially dive bombers like the Stuka), and concentrated force at a single point of breakthrough. Blitzkrieg aimed to encircle and destroy enemy forces before they could mount a coordinated defense. It proved devastatingly effective in the invasions of Poland (1939), France (1940), and the early stages of Operation Barbarossa, but faltered against the vast distances and harsh conditions of the Soviet Union.

#### Vichy France (1940–1944)
The collaborationist French government established after France's defeat and armistice with Germany in June 1940, headquartered in the spa town of Vichy. Led by Marshal Philippe Pétain, the Vichy regime governed the unoccupied southern zone of France and maintained control over French colonial territories. It actively collaborated with Nazi Germany, including participation in the deportation of Jews to concentration and extermination camps. Vichy France remains one of the most contentious chapters in French national memory.

#### Winston Churchill (1874–1965)
British Prime Minister from 1940 to 1945, Churchill became the symbolic leader of Allied resistance against Nazi Germany. His defiant rhetoric (e.g., "We shall fight on the beaches") rallied British morale during the darkest period of the war, particularly during the Battle of Britain and the Blitz. Churchill was instrumental in forging the Grand Alliance with Roosevelt and Stalin, though his relationship with both leaders involved significant strategic disagreements. A complex figure, he was also an ardent imperialist who resisted Indian independence.

#### Battle of Britain and the Blitz (1940–1941)
The Battle of Britain (July–October 1940) was the German air campaign (Luftwaffe) against the Royal Air Force (RAF) to achieve air superiority as a prerequisite for a cross-Channel invasion (Operation Sea Lion). British advantages—including radar technology, Fighter Command's organization, and the advantage of fighting over home territory—allowed the RAF to prevail. The Blitz (September 1940–May 1941) was the subsequent German shift to sustained nighttime bombing of London and other British cities, killing approximately 43,000 civilians. It failed to break British morale or industrial capacity, and Germany shelved invasion plans, turning east.

#### German Invasion of the USSR (Operation Barbarossa, June 1941)
The largest military operation in history, launched on June 22, 1941, with over 3 million Axis soldiers invading the Soviet Union along a 1,800-mile front. Hitler aimed to destroy Soviet military power, capture resources (especially Ukrainian grain and Caucasian oil), and secure Lebensraum. Initial German advances were rapid and devastating, but the campaign stalled before Moscow in winter 1941. The invasion transformed the war into a titanic struggle on the Eastern Front, which would ultimately account for approximately 80% of German military casualties in WWII.

#### The Grand Alliance
The wartime coalition of the United States, Great Britain, and the Soviet Union—three powers with fundamentally different political systems and strategic interests united by the shared goal of defeating the Axis. Key conferences (Tehran, Yalta, Potsdam) attempted to coordinate military strategy and plan the postwar order. Tensions over the second front, Poland's future, and the shape of postwar Europe foreshadowed the Cold War breakdown of the alliance.

#### The Holocaust
The systematic, state-sponsored genocide of approximately six million European Jews by Nazi Germany and its collaborators between 1933 and 1945. The Holocaust also targeted Roma, disabled individuals, homosexuals, political prisoners, and others. It progressed through stages: legal exclusion (Nuremberg Laws), forced emigration, ghettoization, mass shootings by Einsatzgruppen on the Eastern Front, and ultimately industrialized killing in extermination camps (Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, and others). The Holocaust represents an unprecedented act of genocide and fundamentally shaped postwar international law, human rights discourse, and the creation of Israel.

#### Final Solution (Endlösung)
The Nazi plan for the systematic extermination of all European Jews, formalized at the Wannsee Conference in January 1942. Senior Nazi officials coordinated the logistics of transporting Jews from across occupied Europe to extermination camps equipped with gas chambers. The term reflects the Nazis' bureaucratic euphemism for genocide: earlier "solutions" had included forced emigration and ghettoization, but the Final Solution represented the deliberate decision to pursue total annihilation.

#### Firebombing of Dresden (February 1945)
A controversial Allied bombing campaign against the German city of Dresden on February 13–15, 1945, involving British and American air forces. The resulting firestorm killed an estimated 25,000 people and destroyed much of the historic city center. The military necessity of the bombing has been debated ever since: defenders argued Dresden was a legitimate transportation and communications hub; critics contended it was primarily a cultural center with limited military value and that the attack constituted disproportionate destruction of civilian life.

#### D-Day (June 6, 1944)
The Allied amphibious invasion of Normandy, France—Operation Overlord—marking the opening of the long-awaited Western Front (second front) in Europe. Approximately 156,000 Allied troops landed on five beaches (Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, Sword) in the largest seaborne invasion in history. Despite fierce German resistance, particularly at Omaha Beach, the Allies secured a foothold and began the liberation of Western Europe. D-Day fulfilled the Western Allies' commitment to Stalin and began the final push toward Germany from the west.

#### V-E Day (May 8, 1945)
Victory in Europe Day, marking the unconditional surrender of Nazi Germany. Hitler had committed suicide on April 30 in his Berlin bunker; Admiral Karl Dönitz, his designated successor, authorized the surrender. The war in Europe was formally over, though the Pacific war continued until August. V-E Day was celebrated across the Allied world but also marked the beginning of the enormous challenge of postwar reconstruction, displaced populations, and the emerging division of Europe into Western and Soviet spheres.

### Post-WWII Order

#### Yalta Conference (February 1945)
A meeting of the "Big Three"—Roosevelt, Churchill, and Stalin—at the Crimean resort of Yalta to plan the final phase of the war and the postwar order. Key agreements included: the division of Germany into occupation zones, free elections in liberated Eastern European countries (a promise Stalin would not honor), Soviet entry into the Pacific war against Japan, and the establishment of the United Nations. Yalta is often cited as the moment when the postwar division of Europe began to take shape.

#### Division of Germany
After Germany's surrender, the country was divided into four occupation zones controlled by the United States, Britain, France, and the Soviet Union, as agreed at Yalta and Potsdam. Berlin, located deep within the Soviet zone, was similarly divided. By 1949, the three Western zones merged into the Federal Republic of Germany (West Germany), while the Soviet zone became the German Democratic Republic (East Germany). The division of Germany became the most visible symbol of the Iron Curtain and Cold War division of Europe, lasting until reunification in 1990.

#### Nuremberg and Tokyo Trials
The postwar international military tribunals that prosecuted Axis leaders for war crimes, crimes against peace, and crimes against humanity. The Nuremberg Trials (1945–1946) tried 22 major Nazi leaders; 12 were sentenced to death. The Tokyo Trials (1946–1948) prosecuted 28 Japanese leaders, with 7 executed. These trials established crucial precedents in international law: individuals (including heads of state) could be held personally accountable for violations of international law, and "following orders" was not a valid defense. They laid the groundwork for modern international criminal justice, including the International Criminal Court.

#### UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)
A landmark document adopted by the United Nations General Assembly on December 10, 1948, largely drafted by a committee chaired by Eleanor Roosevelt. It proclaimed a set of universal, fundamental human rights to be protected for all people regardless of nationality, including the right to life, liberty, freedom from torture, freedom of expression, and the right to education. While not legally binding, the UDHR established a moral and political framework that influenced subsequent international human rights treaties and national constitutions worldwide. It was a direct response to the atrocities of WWII and the Holocaust.

#### NATO and Warsaw Pact
The two rival military alliances of the Cold War. NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization), established in 1949, was a collective defense pact among Western democracies led by the United States, based on the principle that an attack on one member was an attack on all (Article 5). The Warsaw Pact (1955) was the Soviet-led military alliance of communist Eastern European states, created as a direct counter to NATO and the rearmament of West Germany. Together, they represented the military architecture of the Cold War's bipolar order and the division of Europe into two armed camps.

## 3. Practice Quiz — All Questions

21 AP-style multiple-choice questions covering all four sections. Each question tests historical thinking skills: causation, significance, contextualization, comparison, and argumentation. Questions are designed to go beyond simple recall and require analysis of historical concepts.

### Question 1
**The Mukden Incident of 1931 is historically significant primarily because it**

A) led to the immediate outbreak of World War II in Asia

B) demonstrated the League of Nations' inability to enforce collective security

C) resulted in a formal alliance between Japan and Nazi Germany

D) provoked the United States into imposing an oil embargo on Japan

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Japan's staged attack and subsequent seizure of Manchuria exposed the League of Nations' toothlessness—it condemned Japan but could do nothing to reverse the aggression, emboldening future revisionist powers.

### Question 2
**Which of the following best describes the concept of Lebensraum in Nazi ideology?**

A) The belief that Germany needed to reclaim territories lost in the Treaty of Versailles

B) The doctrine that Aryan racial superiority required the conquest of eastern territories for settlement and resources

C) A diplomatic strategy for negotiating colonial possessions in Africa

D) An economic policy focused on achieving industrial self-sufficiency within existing German borders

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Lebensraum went beyond simple territorial revision. It was a racially motivated vision of expansionist colonization of Eastern Europe, particularly at the expense of Slavic populations, rooted in social Darwinist thinking.

### Question 3
**The significance of the Remilitarization of the Rhineland (1936) was that it**

A) triggered an immediate military response from France and Britain

B) violated the Treaty of Versailles and revealed Western democracies' unwillingness to enforce it by force

C) was the first step in Hitler's plan to unite all German-speaking peoples

D) was secretly supported by the Soviet Union as part of the Nazi-Soviet Pact

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The Anglo-French failure to respond militarily—despite having the capability—validated Hitler's gamble that he could violate Versailles without consequence. It set the pattern for escalating aggression.

### Question 4
**The Nuremberg Laws (1935) are best understood as**

A) international laws created at the postwar Nuremberg Trials

B) economic regulations designed to exclude Jews from German industry

C) legislation that codified racial discrimination and stripped Jews of citizenship rights

D) wartime emergency decrees authorizing the deportation of Jews to camps

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** The Nuremberg Laws transformed antisemitic ideology into legal reality, defining Jewishness by blood and excluding Jews from citizenship. They were a pre-war legislative act, not connected to the postwar trials.

### Question 5
**A historian arguing that the Munich Conference (1938) represented the failure of appeasement would most likely cite which of the following as evidence?**

A) Hitler honored the agreement by not seeking further territorial expansion

B) Czechoslovakia was not represented at the negotiations and Hitler violated the agreement within six months

C) The agreement successfully prevented the outbreak of war for over a decade

D) Britain and France used the time gained to build military superiority over Germany

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Munich is the defining example of appeasement's failure: Czechoslovakia was sacrificed without representation, and Hitler broke his pledge by occupying the rest of Czechoslovakia in March 1939.

### Question 6
**The Nazi-Soviet Non-Aggression Pact (1939) was strategically important primarily because it**

A) created a lasting alliance between Germany and the Soviet Union based on shared ideology

B) allowed Germany to invade Poland without the risk of a two-front war

C) guaranteed Soviet neutrality throughout the entire duration of World War II

D) was the first formal diplomatic agreement between a fascist and communist state

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The pact's primary strategic value for Hitler was eliminating the eastern threat while he moved against Poland. Its secret protocol dividing Eastern Europe also facilitated the Soviet invasion of eastern Poland.

### Question 7
**The Rape of Nanjing (1937–1938) is significant in the broader context of WWII because it**

A) prompted the United States to immediately declare war on Japan

B) demonstrated the extreme brutality of Japan's war in China and remains contested in East Asian historical memory

C) was the first instance of genocide in the twentieth century

D) led directly to the formation of the Allied Powers

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The Nanjing massacre—with an estimated 200,000–300,000 killed—exemplified the scale of violence in the Sino-Japanese War. Its contested legacy continues to affect Sino-Japanese relations today.

### Question 8
**The U.S. strategy of "island hopping" in the Pacific was designed to**

A) invade every Japanese-held island to ensure no enemy forces remained behind Allied lines

B) capture strategically important islands for use as air and naval bases while bypassing heavily fortified positions

C) avoid direct combat with Japanese forces by relying solely on naval blockades

D) focus exclusively on liberating European colonial territories in Southeast Asia

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Island hopping was a strategy of selective engagement—attacking key islands (like Iwo Jima and Okinawa) while bypassing others (like Rabaul) to conserve resources and advance toward Japan more efficiently.

### Question 9
**Which of the following best explains why the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki remain historically controversial?**

**Explanation:** The core historical debate weighs the argument that the bombs shortened the war and avoided a costly invasion against the criticism that they represented unnecessary mass civilian killing, especially given Japan's weakening position.

### Question 10
**Blitzkrieg as a military strategy was most effective when**

A) used in prolonged wars of attrition against well-supplied defenders

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Blitzkrieg depended on speed, surprise, and concentrated force at specific breakthrough points. It worked brilliantly against Poland and France but struggled against the USSR's vast distances and deep reserves.

### Question 12

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** The Eastern Front was the decisive theater of the European war. Roughly 80% of German military casualties occurred there, and the Soviet Union's survival and eventual counteroffensive were essential to Allied victory.

**Explanation:** The Wannsee Conference (January 1942) coordinated the logistics of transporting Jews across occupied Europe to extermination camps. The euphemistic term "Final Solution" masked the reality of industrialized mass murder.

### Question 14

### Question 15
**The Yalta Conference (1945) is often cited as a key moment in the origins of the Cold War because**

A) Roosevelt and Churchill formally declared war on the Soviet Union

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Yalta's promise of free elections in liberated countries masked fundamental disagreements. Stalin's subsequent installation of communist regimes across Eastern Europe violated the spirit of Yalta and deepened East-West mistrust.

### Question 16
**The Nuremberg and Tokyo Trials established which of the following precedents in international law?**

A) Only soldiers, not political leaders, could be prosecuted for actions during wartime

B) Individuals, including heads of state, could be held personally accountable for war crimes and crimes against humanity

C) Nations that lost wars were required to pay unlimited reparations to the victors

D) "Following superior orders" was a complete defense against charges of war crimes

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The trials' most enduring legacy is the principle of individual accountability under international law. The rejection of the "superior orders" defense established that individuals bear moral and legal responsibility for their actions.

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The UDHR was drafted in direct response to the horrors of WWII, establishing universal standards for human dignity that no government should violate—a deliberate attempt to create legal and moral safeguards against future atrocities.

## 4. Feature Specifications

### 4.1 Term Definitions View
- **Search Bar:** Real-time filtering across all term names. As the user types, only matching terms remain visible. Clear button resets the filter.
- **Collapsible Sections:** Each of the four categories (Road to War, Asian Theater, European Theater, Post-WWII Order) is a collapsible accordion. Clicking the section header toggles open/closed. A chevron icon indicates state.
- **Term Cards:** Each term is a clickable card within its section. Clicking expands to reveal the full definition. Only one term per section can be expanded at a time (accordion behavior within sections).
- **Quick Stats:** A small counter at the top showing total terms (35) and the number currently visible based on search.

### 4.2 Quiz View
- **Question Display:** One question at a time, centered on screen. Question number and progress bar shown at top. Four answer options displayed as clickable cards (A, B, C, D).
- **Answer Feedback:** After selecting an answer, the correct option highlights green and the incorrect selection (if wrong) highlights red. A detailed explanation appears below the options. A "Next Question" button advances to the next question.
- **Progress Tracking:** A progress bar at the top fills as questions are completed. Current question number shown (e.g., "7 of 21").
- **Score Summary:** After completing all 21 questions, a summary screen shows: total score (e.g., 17/21 = 81%), a list of all questions with correct/incorrect indicators, the ability to expand any question to review the explanation, and a "Retake Quiz" button that shuffles question order.
- **Quiz Randomization:** Question order is randomized each time the quiz starts. Answer option order within each question is also shuffled to prevent memorization of letter positions.

### 4.3 Navigation & Layout
- **Top Nav Bar:** Fixed at the top. Contains the site title ("WWII Study Guide") and two navigation tabs: "Terms" and "Quiz." Active tab is visually highlighted.
- **Responsive Design:** Content area max-width of ~800px, centered. Cards and buttons are touch-friendly for mobile use. Font sizes adjust for smaller screens.
- **Footer:** Minimal footer with "Unit 4: A World at War — Modern World History" text.
